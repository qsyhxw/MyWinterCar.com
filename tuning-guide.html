2. Rocker Arm Adjustment

Proper rocker arm adjustment is essential for a smooth-running engine. Here's the correct method:

üîß Required Tools
  • Screwdriver ‚Äî for turning the adjustment screw
  • 12mm Wrench or Socket ‚Äî for holding the locknut
1

Start the Engine

IMPORTANT: Rocker arm adjustment must be done with the engine RUNNING. Do not attempt to adjust with the engine off!

2

Access the Rocker Arms

Open the hood and locate the rocker arm cover on top of the engine. You may need to remove the cover to access the adjustment screws.

3

Tighten Until Noise

Using the screwdriver and wrench, tighten each rocker arm adjustment screw until you hear a ticking/tapping noise from that valve.

4

Back Off Slightly

Once you hear the noise, back off the adjustment by just "a tick" (a small amount). The noise should disappear.

5

Repeat for All Valves

Repeat this process for each rocker arm. Listen carefully to ensure all valves are properly adjusted.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes
  • Don't adjust with engine off ‚Äî You need to hear the valves
  • Don't count clicks ‚Äî Listen to the engine, don't rely on counting
  • Don't overtighten ‚Äî This can damage valve components

4. Air-Fuel Ratio (AFR)

The Air-Fuel Ratio (AFR) is crucial for engine performance. Here's what the numbers mean:

AFR Scale

RICH
12.0-13.0
OPTIMAL
13.1-14.7
LEAN
15.0+
More Power / Less Economy More Economy / Risk of Damage
AFR Range Description Use Case
12.0 Very Rich (with nitrous) Extreme performance, short engine life
13.1 Rich (Performance) Racing, maximum power
14.0-14.5 Slightly Rich Normal driving, good balance
14.7 Stoichiometric Best fuel economy, emissions
15.0+ Lean Risk of engine damage, avoid!
üî• Lean = Danger!

Running too lean (high AFR) can cause overheating and engine damage. Always err on the side of slightly rich (lower AFR) if unsure.
